# Adventure Design

This chapter covers how to build stories — from a single session to a year-long chronicle — drawing on techniques from both the Storyteller system and the cinematic DNA of Star Wars.

---

## Chronicle vs. Story

| Term          | Duration         | Contains                                                    |
| ------------- | ---------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Chronicle** | Months to years  | Multiple connected stories, character arcs, a central theme |
| **Story**     | 1–4 sessions     | A complete arc with a beginning, middle, and end            |
| **Chapter**   | A single session | One or more scenes                                          |

---

## The Five Elements of a Story

Every story is built from five parts. Think about each one before you start writing.

### Theme

The theme is what the story is _about_ — not the plot, but the question it asks. Express it as a question you never answer directly:

- _What does it cost to do the right thing?_
- _Can a soldier escape their past?_
- _Is the Dark Side a choice or a destiny?_

The theme guides you when you're unsure what should happen next. A story about redemption plays differently than a story about survival.

### Conflict

Conflict drives the story forward. Every scene should involve at least one active conflict, even if it's internal. Star Wars conflicts map well to this framework:

| Conflict Type            | Star Wars Example  | VtM Parallel           |
| ------------------------ | ------------------ | ---------------------- |
| **Rebels vs. Empire**    | Any Rebel cell     | Anarchs vs. Elders     |
| **Jedi vs. Sith**        | Light vs. Dark     | Humanity vs. the Beast |
| **Smuggler vs. Hutt**    | Han and Jabba      | Kindred vs. Prince     |
| **Survival vs. Justice** | Rogue One          | Survival vs. Justice   |
| **Friend vs. Friend**    | Obi-Wan vs. Anakin | Clan vs. Clan          |

A good story has layers — one obvious conflict (what the characters think is happening) and one real conflict (what is actually going on).

### Drama

Drama is what makes a scene memorable. To create it:

- **Mix action with something else** — not just a firefight, but a firefight in a collapsing hangar with civilians in the way.
- **Raise stakes continuously** — every success should reveal a new complication.
- **Make scenes distinct** — each one should have something the players will remember.

### Plot

The plot is the sequence of events. A well-structured plot follows these stages:

| Stage                 | Purpose                                                                                                                                                         | How Long                  |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **Setting the Scene** | Describe where and when. Use all five senses. Let players interact right away.                                                                                  | 5–10 minutes              |
| **Hook**              | Present the conflict in a compelling way. Something is wrong, danger awaits, a threat hangs over the characters. Draw them in immediately or they'll check out. | First scene               |
| **Buildup**           | Escalate tension. Give the characters obstacles to overcome and clues to find. Multiple paths to the conclusion. Keep the pressure constant.                    | 60–80% of session time    |
| **Cliffhanger**       | End a chapter on a unresolved note — a revelation, a betrayal, a door opening. Makes sure they come back.                                                       | End of each session       |
| **Plot Twist**        | Change direction mid-stream. An ally is an enemy. The mission was a setup. The real threat was never what they thought.                                         | Any point                 |
| **Climax**            | All questions answered (at least some). Must evolve naturally from the characters' actions — never pre-ordained.                                                | Final scene               |
| **Resolution**        | Wind down. Reward the players — new information, resources, closure. Set up the next story. Keep it short.                                                      | 5–10 minutes after climax |

### Mood

Mood is the emotional tone. Set it early — start with something that captures the feel you want.

| Mood           | Star Wars Vibe                | How to Create It                                           |
| -------------- | ----------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Heroic**     | Original Trilogy              | Uplifting music cues, narrow escapes, witty banter         |
| **Desperate**  | Rogue One, Rebels S1          | Losses that stick, moral compromises, scarce resources     |
| **Mysterious** | The Force Awakens (first act) | Fog, shadows, unanswered questions, unreliable information |
| **Brooding**   | Empire Strikes Back           | Oppressive atmosphere, bad news, impending doom            |
| **Triumphant** | Return of the Jedi (end)      | Victories earned through sacrifice, catharsis              |

## The Prelude (Session Zero)

A prelude is a solo session before the chronicle begins. It gives each character a past so the future has weight.

Run a prelude to answer:

1. **Who was this character before the story started?** A soldier, a slicer, a scholar?
2. **How did they get their skills?** Self-taught, formal training, street experience?
3. **Who do they know?** Contacts, rivals, debts, allies.
4. **What do they want?** A goal that will drive them through the chronicle.
5. **What are they afraid of?** A flaw, a secret, a failure.

The prelude is one-on-one. Let the player make choices freely. Don't force combat — let them discover who their character is. Let them change Traits during the prelude if the story reveals something new.

If there's no time for a full prelude, at least answer the five questions collaboratively before the first session.

## Building a Star Wars Story

### Story Hooks

A hook must be immediate and personal. The players should know something is wrong within the first exchange.

| Category          | Example                                                                                                |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Distress call** | A Rebel cell's signal goes dark. Last transmission: "They found us."                                   |
| **Bounty**        | A familiar face appears on a wanted poster — with a price that could change your life.                 |
| **Mystery**       | A Jedi artifact turns up in a junk shop. The seller doesn't remember how they got it.                  |
| **Debt**          | A contact calls in a favor. "You owe me. I'm collecting."                                              |
| **Disaster**      | A Star Destroyer drops out of hyperspace over the planet you're on — and it's not supposed to be here. |

### Pacing

| Pace         | Story Beats per Session                                    | Best For                          |
| ------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| **Fast**     | Hook → Buildup → Climax → Resolution in one session        | One-shots, action-heavy episodes  |
| **Moderate** | Hook at end of session 1, Buildup through 2–3, Climax at 4 | Standard stories                  |
| **Slow**     | Full act per session (3–4 sessions per story)              | Investigation, political intrigue |

### Star Wars-Specific Conflict Templates

| Template              | Tone                   | Example                                                    |
| --------------------- | ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **The Heist**         | Fast, clever           | Steal Imperial codes from a secure facility                |
| **The Chase**         | Urgent, escalating     | Flee across the galaxy with the enemy one step behind      |
| **The Defense**       | Desperate, heroic      | Hold the line until civilians evacuate                     |
| **The Investigation** | Slow, mysterious       | Uncover a conspiracy in a crime lord's organization        |
| **The Duel**          | Personal, emotional    | Jedi vs. Sith — the fate of a student hangs in the balance |
| **The Escape**        | Tense, resource-scarce | Break out of an Imperial prison with nothing but your wits |

## Session Zero Checklist

Before the first session, make sure you have:

- [ ] **Setting** — Where does the chronicle begin? What does the city/planet feel like?
- [ ] **Group cohesion** — Why are these characters together? What binds them?
- [ ] **Primary antagonists** — Who opposes them? What do the antagonists want?
- [ ] **Central theme** — What question does the chronicle ask?
- [ ] **Tone** — Cinematic, Balanced, or Brutal? (See [Difficulty Reference](./difficulty-reference.mdx))
- [ ] **Character web** — How do the PCs know each other? Are there existing relationships, debts, rivalries?
- [ ] **First hook** — How does session 1 start? Make it active and immediate.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Rigid plot** — Never force the players to follow a pre-written script. Have situations, not a sequence. Let the characters' choices determine what happens.
- **No stakes** — If failure doesn't matter, don't roll. If success is guaranteed, don't roll.
- **Everyone agrees** — Not all characters should want the same thing. Internal disagreement creates drama.
- **One solution** — Always have multiple paths to the goal. If the players miss the clue, they should be able to find another way.
- **Powerful NPCs solving everything** — The PCs are the protagonists. NPCs advise, hinder, and flavor — they don't rescue.
