# Building Encounters

Every encounter is an obstacle between the characters and their goal. This chapter covers how to build encounters — from mook-filled firefights to tense social negotiations — using the tools from both the Storyteller system and this conversion.

---

## NPC Tiers

Not all NPCs are created equal. The system uses three tiers to keep combat fast and dramatic.

### Named NPCs (Antagonists)

Named NPCs use the **full character creation rules**. They have Attributes, Abilities, Virtues, Willpower, Force Points, and a full 7-level Health track. They can soak Lethal damage, just like PCs.

Use named NPCs for:

- **Recurring villains** (a rival bounty hunter, an Imperial officer, a Dark Jedi)
- **Boss encounters** (a Hutt crime lord, a witch-hunter, a Sith acolyte)
- **Allies and rivals** who matter to the story

### Unnamed NPCs (Fodder)

Unnamed NPCs use a simplified stat block — a few key Traits and a shared health pool. They **cannot soak Lethal damage**. Every successful hit takes them down a health level. This keeps fights fast and lets heroes feel powerful.

| Trait             | Typical Fodder                   | Tough Fodder              |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **Dice pools**    | 3–5D                             | 5–7D                      |
| **Health levels** | 3 (Hurt, Injured, Incapacitated) | 5 (full track to Wounded) |
| **Soak Lethal**   | No                               | No                        |
| **Willpower**     | 3                                | 4                         |

Use fodder for:

- **Stormtroopers** (surprisingly inaccurate, but dangerous in numbers)
- **Street thugs, pirates, mercenaries**
- **TIE fighter pilots** — inconsequential vehicles don't get soak rolls either

### Creatures

Creatures follow the [Creature mechanics](../creatures/mechanics.mdx) chapter. They have simplified stats with scale modifiers and special abilities. Some are fodder (rat, mynock), some are named-tier threats (rancor, krayt dragon).

## Action Types

Every encounter resolves through dice rolls. The Storyteller chooses which action type fits the situation.

### Simple Actions

A **simple action** needs only one success to succeed. Most combat attacks, routine skill checks, and quick tasks use simple actions.

_Roll once. One success is enough._

**Automatic success:** If the dice pool equals or exceeds the difficulty, the character succeeds automatically without rolling. This is for actions the character could perform in their sleep.

A player can spend a **Willpower point** to claim one automatic success on any single roll.

### Extended Actions

An **extended action** requires accumulating multiple successes over several rolls. Use extended actions for tasks where time pressure or botch risk creates drama.

| Task                                       | Required Successes |
| ------------------------------------------ | ------------------ |
| Climbing a tree                            | 7                  |
| Repairing Light vehicle damage (permanent) | 5 × scale          |
| Reprogramming a droid                      | 3–5                |
| Researching a data archive                 | 5–15               |

The character rolls each turn (or hour, for downtime tasks) until they accumulate the required successes. A **botch** resets progress to zero, and the Storyteller may rule the task cannot be retried.

### Resisted Actions

In a **resisted action**, two characters roll against each other. The one with more successes wins. The opponent's successes cancel the winner's excess — so outstanding successes are rare.

**Examples:** Shadowing a target, arm wrestling, a debate, a chase.

### Extended & Resisted Actions

Both characters accumulate successes over time. The first to reach a target number wins. Each turn, only net successes (yours minus theirs) count toward the total.

**Example:** A cinematic duel, a hacking race, a pursuit through the Coruscant underworld.

## Setting Difficulty

### Standard Difficulty

The baseline difficulty for most rolls is **6**. See the **[Difficulty & Success Reference](./difficulty-reference.mdx)** for the full ladder (Diff 3–10) and interactive probability calculator.

### Modifying Difficulty

Apply modifiers based on the situation:

| Circumstance                    | Modifier                |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Ideal conditions                | −1 to −2                |
| Good tools available            | −1 to −2                |
| Adverse conditions              | +1 to +2                |
| Poor or no tools                | +1 to +3                |
| Opponent has relevant Specialty | +1                      |
| Scale difference (per step)     | ±1 to hit, ∓1 to damage |

### Setting Difficulty for Dramatic Effect

Not all encounters should be evenly balanced. The difficulty does not need to be the same for everyone. A Royal Guard with a force pike has an advantage over a smuggler with a vibro-knife. The Moff in his fortified keep knows the terrain better than the invading Rebels.

When setting difficulty, consider:

1. **Terrain and environment** — does one side have home turf advantage?
2. **Equipment disparity** — military-grade vs. civilian gear
3. **Preparation** — ambushes vs. reacting
4. **Morale and motivation** — a desperate defender fights harder

## Balancing Encounters

### The 80/20 Rule

About 80% of gameplay is handled by 20% of the rules. Most encounters don't need stat blocks — a difficulty number and a description are enough. Reserve full stats for **boss fights** and **pivotal scenes**.

### Numbers Advantage

In the WoD system, numbers matter. A group of 4–6 fodder NPCs can threaten a PC because the dice will eventually favor the attackers. Use the **rule of 1** (botch chance) to let PCs feel heroic even when outnumbered.

### Scale and Power

Use [Combat Scales](../combat/combat-scales.mdx) when mixing creatures, vehicles, and characters. A single step of scale represents a massive power difference — a rancor (Scale 2) against a character (Scale 0) is nearly invulnerable to small arms.

### Threat Level Guide

| Encounter       | Fodder | Named NPCs   | Difficulty |
| --------------- | ------ | ------------ | ---------- |
| **Easy**        | 2–3    | 0            | 5–6        |
| **Standard**    | 4–6    | 0–1          | 6          |
| **Challenging** | 6–10   | 1–2          | 6–7        |
| **Hard**        | 8–12   | 2–3          | 7–8        |
| **Boss**        | 0–4    | 1 (powerful) | 8–9        |

These are loose guidelines. Let the story dictate the encounter, not the math.

### The Heroic Soak Rule

PCs and named NPCs always lose at least **1 Health level** from any attack that deals Lethal damage, regardless of soak. This prevents any character from becoming invincible. Fodder NPCs cannot soak Lethal damage at all.

## Social Encounters

Social encounters follow the same action system. Use **Charisma + appropriate Ability** for persuasion, **Manipulation + Intimidation** for threats, **Appearance + Expression** for performance.

| Goal                             | Dice Pool                 | Difficulty                                  |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Convince a guard to let you pass | Manipulation + Subterfuge | 6 (guard is loyal) to 8 (Imperial garrison) |
| Haggle for a lower price         | Charisma + Streetwise     | +1 per 10% discount                         |
| Intimidate for information       | Strength + Intimidation   | Opponent's Willpower                        |
| Impress at a social function     | Appearance + Etiquette    | 6 (casual) to 8 (Imperial gala)             |

### Social Combat

For extended social conflicts (debates, interrogations, courtroom dramas), use the **Extended & Resisted** action model. Each side accumulates successes. The first to reach the opponent's Willpower wins the argument.

## Vehicle Encounters

Refer to the [Space & Vehicle Combat](../vehicles-mechanisms/space-combat.mdx) chapter for full rules. Quick guidelines:

- **Inconsequential vehicles** (unnamed TIE fighters, speeder bikes) do not get soak rolls
- **Scale difference** affects to-hit and damage difficulty by ±1 per step
- **Capital ships** use Wits + Command to determine actions per turn
- **Chases** use range bands — the prey escapes by accumulating enough Maneuver successes

## The 80/20 Principle in Practice

The most important tool in your kit is knowing when **not** to roll. If the outcome is certain and failure isn't interesting, just describe it and move on.

| Situation                                                           | Roll?                                          |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| A skilled pilot flying through an open hangar bay                   | No — automatic success                         |
| The same pilot dodging debris in a collapsing space station         | Yes — difficulty 7                             |
| A PC tries to recall basic Imperial procedure                       | No — they know it                              |
| A PC tries to recall the access codes for a specific Star Destroyer | Yes — Intelligence + Bureaucracy, difficulty 8 |

## Complications and Botches

A **botch** (zero successes + at least one 1 rolled) should never just mean "you fail." It should introduce a **complication** that makes the scene more interesting.

### Combat Botches

| Roll   | Complication                                  |
| ------ | --------------------------------------------- |
| Attack | Weapon jam, dropped weapon, hit an ally       |
| Dodge  | Stumble, fall prone, drop guard               |
| Pilot  | Collision risk, system flicker, lose position |

### Social Botches

| Roll         | Complication                             |
| ------------ | ---------------------------------------- |
| Persuasion   | Offend the target, reveal too much       |
| Intimidation | Target calls your bluff, security called |
| Stealth      | Knock something over, alarm triggered    |

### Extended Action Botches

On a botch during an extended action, the character **loses all accumulated successes**. The Storyteller may rule that:

- The task cannot be attempted again without new tools or information
- A complication makes the situation worse (e.g., the engine now has additional damage)
- Time runs out (the door opens, the Imperials arrive)

## Teamwork

Two or more characters can work together on the same task. Each rolls separately and **adds their successes together**. They never add dice pools together into one roll.

Teamwork works well for:

- Lifting heavy objects (Strength + Athletics)
- Repairing a vehicle (Intelligence + Tech)
- Researching a topic (Intelligence + Investigation)
- Covering fire (the shooter gets bonus dice from the covering character's successes)

Teamwork hinders in some contexts. Multiple people trying to convince the same target may confuse them. Use judgment.
