# Running the Game

This chapter covers the practical side of being a Gamemaster — making rulings, managing players, pacing sessions, and knowing when to follow the rules and when to break them.

---

## The 80/20 Principle

About 80% of gameplay is handled by 20% of the rules. Most encounters don't need stat blocks — a difficulty number and a description are enough. Reserve full stat blocks for **boss fights** and **pivotal scenes**.

When you find yourself pausing the game to look up a rule, ask: _does this ruling matter to the story right now?_ If the answer is no, make a call and move on. Note it for later if you want to check the actual rule between sessions.

### What You Always Need

- **Difficulty** — Pick a number from the ladder (see [Difficulty Reference](./difficulty-reference.mdx))
- **Description** — What does success look like? What does failure cost?
- **Consequences** — What happens when the dice turn against them?

### What You Rarely Need

- Full NPC stat blocks for random encounters
- Exact range tables for every weapon
- Rules for obscure environmental conditions

At the table, speed matters more than precision. The game should never stop while you flip pages.

## Making Rulings

Every Gamemaster develops a personal style. The extremes are useful to recognize, even if most of us end up somewhere in between.

### The Rules Lawyer

This GM insists on dice rolls for everything and applies the letter of the rules to the fullest extent. The world feels self-consistent, and players trust that their choices have mechanical weight.

**Works well for:** Tactical groups, one-shots, players who enjoy optimizing.

**Danger:** The game slows down. Drama gets buried under modifiers.

### The Freeformer

This GM treats the story as everything. Dice are rolled only occasionally; the GM usually decides what happens based on narrative logic. Character actions direct the story, and the GM follows.

**Works well for:** Improvisational groups, horror, political intrigue.

**Danger:** Players feel their character sheet doesn't matter. Choices lose mechanical weight.

### Finding Your Balance

This conversion leans toward the freeform end. The rules are tools, not a straitjacket. Your goal is maximum player freedom of action — players should never feel that their decisions and choices don't make a difference.

### When to Break the Rules

From time to time, situations arise where applying the full letter of the rules would cause the story to slow or collapse.

**A character carries a vital artifact. A freak botch sends them falling into a fire. The artifact is lost. The story cannot continue.**

You have options:

| Option                    | Approach                                                                                        | Risk                                                  |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| **Discount the botch**    | Reduce the severity — the artifact is saved, the character lands on a ledge                     | Works once. Players learn you'll save them every time |
| **Amend the story**       | There's another source for the information. A rival has a copy, or a contact knows the contents | Players may wait for you to spoon-feed them           |
| **Allow a heroic effort** | Let the character throw the artifact to a comrade at the last moment                            | Some players may be too selfish to make this choice   |
| **Let it stand**          | The chronicle takes a new direction. The characters must find another way                       | Powerful lesson that actions have consequences        |

None of these are wrong. Your job is to decide which fits the tone of your game. Sometimes the best choice is to let the story fall apart and rebuild from the wreckage.

### Guiding Principles for Rulings

1. **Never take away the players' free will** — They need real choices, not the illusion of them
2. **Don't force a predetermined plot** — Weave the story around the characters, don't stick them into yours
3. **Don't let luck rule the plot** — Give players chances to make their Traits and skill matter. Automatic successes are a great tool for this
4. **Don't ignore the characters' Traits** — A botched trap because you forgot a PC has Danger Sense breaks trust
5. **Know your players' Traits before the session** — Read their sheets. Make notes of what you need to remember

## Player Management

### What Players Want

Players come to the table for different reasons. Understanding their motivations helps you entertain them:

| Motivation          | What They Want                                   | How to Feed It                                                                                  |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Story**           | To tell a beautiful story, a work of art         | Give them dramatic hooks, moral choices, consequences that echo                                 |
| **Escapism**        | To be someone completely different               | Let them act out the role. Give them in-character moments that have nothing to do with the plot |
| **Problem-solving** | Puzzles, riddles, mental dilemmas                | Plant mysteries, layered conspiracies, locked-room problems                                     |
| **Accomplishment**  | The satisfying feeling of completing a hard task | Reward clever planning. Let them feel their XP has paid off                                     |
| **Power**           | To gain influence and control                    | Channel this into responsibility — leadership roles, command, political weight                  |
| **Socializing**     | To be with friends                               | Keep the atmosphere light. Don't let the game become a chore                                    |
| **Emotion**         | To feel fear, grief, joy, catharsis              | Raise stakes. Create moments that matter emotionally, not just mechanically                     |

The same player may want different things on different nights. Pay attention.

### Spotlight Management

Even the most mild-mannered players can get excited and try to dominate. Strong personalities can overshadow quieter ones. Over time, players who feel ignored will drop out.

- **Listen equally** to all players. If one player states what another's character is doing, check with that player first
- **Redirect dominating players** diplomatically. Remind everyone this is not a competitive game
- **Ask quiet players directly** — "What does your character do while the others argue?"
- **Build scenes around specific characters** — a contact only one PC knows, a skill only one PC has
- **Take breaks** — let players roleplay among themselves while you step back

### Character Cohesion

Before the chronicle begins, make sure the characters have a reason to work together. Ask:

- Why are these characters together?
- What binds them — friendship, debt, shared goal, common enemy?
- Does each character have a role in the group?

If players create characters in isolation, you risk a group of anarchists who can't agree on anything. Use the [Session Zero checklist from Adventure Design](./adventure-design.mdx#session-zero-checklist) to head this off.

## Session Management

### The Arc of a Session

A well-paced session follows a natural rhythm:

1. **Open** (5–10 min) — Recap last session. Set the scene. Present a hook immediately
2. **Rising action** (60–70% of session) — Escalate tension. Introduce complications. Let the dice fall
3. **Climax** (the big scene) — The central conflict resolves (or doesn't)
4. **Wind-down** (5–10 min) — Let the dust settle. Award XP. Tease what's next

### Pacing Tools

**Timing** — If the players spend too long debating, create an event that interrupts them. Let the action come to them if they don't go to it.

**Deadlines** — "The bomb goes off in 30 minutes." A ticking clock creates urgency. Adjust the deadline to match the drama — let them succeed if they've earned it.

**Changes of pace** — Not every scene needs to be tense. Give players moments to relax, then hit them harder. Contrast makes the scary parts scarier.

**Description** — Use vivid sensory detail to build atmosphere. A lavish description of a rotting mansion before the players enter it builds dread. Alternately, leave things undescribed and let their imaginations fill the void.

**Scope** — Start small and expand, or start big and focus. Star Wars itself uses this — the Death Star's impossible size makes the human drama feel insignificant at first, then the focus narrows.

### Taking Breaks

You, the GM, almost never get a break while the players rest. Schedule short breaks during natural lulls. A good technique: start the players roleplaying among themselves before you step away. If they're engaged with each other, they won't be bored while you're not involved.

### When the Dice Betray the Story

A single bad roll can derail hours of planning. Before it happens, decide your philosophy:

- **Fail forward** — The characters fail, but the story continues. They don't pick the lock? They get caught, and now it's a prison break
- **Escalate, don't negate** — A botch doesn't mean nothing happens. It means something worse happens. The door isn't just locked — an alarm goes off and a patrol is inbound
- **Give them an out** — There's always another path, even if it's harder. The players should never feel trapped by a single die roll

## Difficulty Calibration

The **two-knob system** (Difficulty + Success Threshold) gives you fine control over tone.

See the full [Difficulty & Success Reference](./difficulty-reference.mdx) for the interactive calculator, but as a quick guide:

| Tone          | Diff | Threshold | How It Feels                                   |
| ------------- | ---- | --------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| **Cinematic** | 4–6  | 1         | PCs mostly succeed. Drama comes from the story |
| **Balanced**  | 6–7  | 1–2       | Skill matters. PCs sweat the hard rolls        |
| **Brutal**    | 7–10 | 3+        | Only specialists can achieve true success      |

**Adjust per scene, not per chronicle.** A cinematic game can have brutal moments, and a brutal game needs an occasional win.

## Tone, Genre & Star Wars

This conversion is built for the fast, pulpy action of the Star Wars galaxy. Keep these principles in mind:

- **Heroes are competent** — Dice pools of 5–8 are normal for starting specialists. Don't make them roll for things their character could do in their sleep
- **Failure is interesting** — A failed roll doesn't mean "nothing happens." It means something goes wrong, complicating the characters' lives
- **NPCs serve the story** — Named NPCs can be brilliant, terrifying, or tragic. Unnamed NPCs are speed bumps and cannon fodder
- **Scale is spectacle** — Use the scale system to let characters punch above their weight. A proton torpedo shot against a Death Star is nearly impossible — and that's why it's worth rolling
- **Star Wars is hopeful** — Even the darkest stories in this galaxy end with a spark of hope. Let the characters earn their victories
