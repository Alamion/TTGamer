# Dice Pools: The Core Engine

Every action under pressure — from shooting a blaster to slicing an Imperial terminal — resolves through a single unified mechanic: **the dice pool**. This document covers everything about how dice work in the system, from the simplest marginal check to cinematic opposed struggles.

## 1. The Core Loop

When your character attempts something risky or tactical, follow this sequence:

1. **Build your pool** — Add the relevant **Attribute** dots to the relevant **Ability** (Skill/Talent/Knowledge) dots. The result is your total dice pool.
2. **Roll d10s** — Roll that many ten-sided dice. The Game Master sets a **Difficulty** (the number each die must meet or beat to count as a success). The standard baseline is **6**.
3. **Apply the Rule of One** — Every die showing a `1` cancels exactly one success. Remove both the cancelled success and the `1` from your evaluation.
4. **Count successes** — Each remaining die that meets or exceeds the difficulty counts as one success. One success is usually enough for standard tasks.

<TWWrapper>
    <InlineRoll notation={'5d10\u003e=6f=1@2,4,7,8,1'} variant="details" preroll />
</TWWrapper>

In this roll of 5d10: the `7` and `8` are successes (2 total), but the `1` cancels one — leaving **1 success**. A marginal victory. The lock cycles open just as the patrol passes.

:::info[System Identity]
This hybrid uses d10 dice pools with variable difficulty and the Rule of One, inherited from **Vampire: The Masquerade 2nd Edition**. Unlike modern WoD games, difficulty fluctuates, ones punish, and botches are a real threat.
:::

## 2. Difficulty & Target Numbers

The Storyteller sets a difficulty between **3** and **10**. This number represents how hard the task is given the circumstances — not your character's skill. A master slicer still rolls against difficulty 6 to crack a standard lock; their skill just means they roll more dice.

### The Difficulty Ladder

| Diff   | Label                  | Why This Label?                                                                                                                             | Example Situations                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| ------ | ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **3**  | Easy                   | A gentle breeze couldn't fail. You're acting without pressure, perfect conditions, plenty of time.                                          | - Replacing a power cell on a blaster you've owned for years<br/>- Recalling the name of a famous Senator from your homeworld<br/>- Walking across a stable catwalk in good lighting                                                                                            |
| **4**  | Routine                | You've done this a hundred times. Your muscle memory can carry you through minor distractions.                                              | - Piloting a landspeeder down an empty straight spaceport lane<br/>- Serving drinks at a busy cantina — you've slung a thousand glasses<br/>- Decrypting a standard-format cargo manifest                                                                                       |
| **5**  | Straightforward        | The procedure is clear. No surprises, but it requires actual focus. A rookie could do it with guidance.                                     | - Slicing a low-grade commercial security lock<br/>- Applying a field dressing to a clean blaster graze<br/>- Hot-wiring a civilian speeder with standard ignition                                                                                                              |
| **6**  | **Standard (default)** | Most regular galactic tasks under tactical pressure. This is the baseline for everything that matters.                                      | - Hitting a stormtrooper at moderate range in a firefight<br/>- Dodging incoming blaster bolts<br/>- Jumping across a gap while being shot at<br/>- Persuading a guard to let you pass                                                                                          |
| **7**  | Challenging            | The situation fights back. Bad lighting, shaky footing, active resistance. You need real focus.                                             | - Searching an unlit garbage chute for hidden codes<br/>- Shooting at a partially covered target<br/>- Negotiating with a hostile official who holds all the cards<br/>- Repairing a hyperdrive motivator while the ship is shaking from turbolaser impacts                     |
| **8**  | Difficult              | Most people would fail. The environment, the equipment, or the opponent is actively working against your success.                           | - Landing a clean shot on a fast-moving speeder bike<br/>- Tracking a quarry through a driving rainstorm at night<br/>- Bypassing a military-grade security system<br/>- Convincing a skeptical Moff to spare your crew                                                         |
| **9**  | Extremely Difficult    | You're attempting something that the galaxy considers almost foolhardy. Pure talent won't save you — you need the Force or phenomenal luck. | - Maintaining supreme focus while heavy ordnance detonates three meters away<br/>- Sniping a thermal exhaust port two meters wide<br/>- Piloting through a collapsing asteroid field<br/>- Talking your way past a bounty hunter who has already collected your file            |
| **10** | Nearly Impossible      | This is the kind of thing that epic songs are written about — if anyone lives to sing them.                                                 | - Breaking down a reinforced blast door with raw physical strength<br/>- Convincing Darth Vader to spare your life through sheer presence<br/>- Landing a crippled starfighter with no thrusters and half a wing<br/>- Resisting direct Force torture from a trained Inquisitor |

:::tip[Difficulty 2]
A difficulty of 2 is so trivial that you should generally let the player succeed automatically. Reserve 2 for situations where botching is narratively interesting even on a trivial task — like a protocol droid tripping while serving drinks to an Imperial Admiral.
:::

:::note[Ten Always Succeeds]
A natural roll of **10** is always a success, regardless of the difficulty. Even at difficulty 10, a die that shows a 10 is a success. A 1 still cancels one success as usual — the 10's success is not immune to the Rule of One.
:::

### Degrees of Success

Getting more than one success means you performed notably better. The same task with different result levels produces very different outcomes:

| Result      | Successes | Degree       | Meaning                                                   | Slicing Example                                                                                        |
| ----------- | --------- | ------------ | --------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Botch       | —         | —            | Catastrophe — action fails and creates a worse situation. | Terminal electrocutes you (1 Lethal) and triggers a **lockdown alarm**.                                |
| Fail        | 0         | —            | Nothing happens. No progress, no penalty.                 | `ACCESS DENIED`. Door stays shut. Try again — but you've wasted time.                                  |
| Marginal    | **1**     | Bare minimum | You squeak by. No extra benefit.                          | Door opens. You step through. Alive. That's what matters.                                              |
| Moderate    | **2**     | Solid        | Clean execution with room to spare.                       | Door opens + you **suppress the access log**.                                                          |
| Complete    | **3**     | Full success | Unambiguous win. Everything you wanted.                   | Door opens, log wiped, you **grab the detention floorplan**.                                           |
| Exceptional | **4**     | Standout     | Lasting advantage. GM should grant a tangible bonus.      | All above + you **implant a backdoor** to monitor this terminal for the session.                       |
| Phenomenal  | **5+**    | Legendary    | The outcome ripples beyond the moment.                    | You own the system + **broadcast a general override** — every door on the level opens, creating chaos. |

### Automatic Success

If your **total dice pool is equal to or greater than the difficulty**, you can skip the roll entirely. The action succeeds as one marginal success — you did it, nothing fancy.

Useful for routine actions under pressure — e.g., _Jax Vorn_ hot-wiring a speeder (diff 5) with Dexterity 3 + Security 3 = 6 dice. The GM doesn't make him roll; he just describes how fast the ignition sparks.

You may **choose to roll anyway** for a shot at more successes (and the risk of a botch). A master slicer with 10 dice against diff 6 might roll hoping for 4+ successes and those exceptional benefits.

## 3. The Rule of One & Botches

Ones are not just low rolls — they actively punish you.

- Each `1` cancels out one success (removing both from the count).
- If **any ones remain after cancelling all successes**, you have **botched** — a catastrophic failure.

<TWWrapper>
    <InlineRoll notation={'5d10\u003e=6f=1@1,3,1,9,1'} variant="details" preroll />
</TWWrapper>

This roll: `9` is one success, but three `1`s cancel it — leaving 0 successes with ones remaining. **Botch!** The lock panel explodes in a shower of sparks, welding the door shut and alerting every guard on the level.

### Botch vs. Failure

- **0 successes, 0 ones remaining** = plain failure. The lock doesn't open. The blaster shot misses. Nothing worse happens.
- **0 successes, 1+ ones remaining** = botch. Your blaster jams, you trip an alarm, you drop the thermal detonator.

:::caution[The Botch Guardrail]
If your successes are cancelled down to exactly **0** by ones (no excess ones), that is a regular failure — not a botch. A botch requires at least one uncancelled one. Example: you roll two 7s and two 1s. Each `1` cancels one success: `2 successes - 2 ones = 0 successes, 0 ones left`. That's a **failure**, not a botch.
:::

## 4. Action Types

Not every roll works the same way. The Storyteller chooses the appropriate framework for the situation.

### Simple Action

One roll, one result. This covers most combat attacks, single skill checks, and snap decisions. Need **at least 1 success** to succeed.

<TWWrapper>
    <CharRoll attr="Dexterity" skill="Blaster" />
</TWWrapper>

### Extended Action

For tasks too complex for a single roll — repairing a hyperdrive, slicing an Imperial mainframe, climbing a cliff. You roll **repeatedly** across turns (or minutes/hours), accumulating successes toward a target total.

- Each roll adds successes to the running total.
- 0 successes = no progress this turn.
- A botch can erase progress, cause injury, or destroy equipment.
- Some tasks are indefinite; others have a hard time limit.

**Examples:**

| Task                        | Target | Pool                         | Diff | Interval    |
| --------------------------- | ------ | ---------------------------- | ---- | ----------- |
| Repair hyperdrive motivator | 15     | Dexterity + Repair           | 7    | 1 roll/hour |
| Climb 50m cliff under fire  | 10     | Strength + Athletics         | 6    | 1 roll/~30s |
| Research lost Rebel cache   | 20     | Intelligence + Investigation | 8    | 1 roll/hour |

The **Degree** of an extended action depends on how many total successes you accumulate relative to the target:

| Performance         | Meaning                                                                                                       |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Barely reach target | You did it, but barely — the hyperdrive works but vibrates loudly; the climb leaves you exhausted             |
| Double the target   | You did it efficiently — the hyperdrive runs quieter than before; you find a safe ledge to rest on the way up |
| Triple the target   | Masterful execution — the hyperdrive is now optimized; you discover a hidden cave during your climb           |

### Resisted Action

When two characters directly oppose each other — hiding vs. spotting, hacking vs. counter-hacking, intimidation vs. resolve — both roll and compare successes.

- Your opponent's successes cancel your own, just like ones do.
- Only the **margin** (your successes minus theirs) counts toward the effect.
- Even if you fail, you can diminish your opponent's result.

<TWWrapper>
    <CharRoll attr="Wits" skill="Stealth" variant="details" /> vs.{' '}
    <CharRoll attr="Perception" skill="Alertness" variant="details" />
</TWWrapper>

**Example:** _Jax_ rolls 3 successes on Stealth. The guard rolls 2 successes on Alertness. Margin = 1 — Jax slips past, but the guard turns around a heartbeat later, suspicious. If the guard had rolled 4 successes, Jax would be spotted.

### Extended + Resisted

Both characters accumulate successes over multiple turns, each trying to reach a target total before the other. The target is often based on the opponent's **Willpower**.

This is used for:

- **Cinematic duels** — blades locked, each duelist feels out the other's defenses. First to accumulate [opponent's Willpower] successes lands a decisive blow.
- **Hacking battles** — slicer vs. security AI. First to accumulate 10 successes wins control of the system.
- **Pursuits** — chasing through a crowded spaceport. First to accumulate 8 successes either catches the target or escapes.

### Teamwork

Multiple characters can contribute to a single extended task. Each rolls separately and their successes are added together.

**When teamwork works:**

- **Repairs** — one character holds the conduit open, another solders. Two engineers can rebuild a hyperdrive faster than one.
- **Research** — three characters comb different archive sections simultaneously.
- **Coordinated actions** — lifting a heavy object, performing a group ritual, navigating a starship through a hazard field.

**When teamwork doesn't work (or makes things worse):**

- **Social situations** — too many voices confuse the subject. One person speaks; the rest provide moral support at best.
- **Fine manipulation** — only one person can fit their hands into the access panel.
- **Stealth** — more people means more noise, more shadows, more chances to slip up.

**How many can help?** The GM decides. A good rule of thumb: **2–3 is optimal**, 4+ starts causing penalties as people get in each other's way. Some tasks (like lifting) scale linearly with strong backs; others (like surgery) allow only one assistant.

## 5. Spending for Success

Heroes can push beyond normal limits.

### Force Points

Spending a Force Point before a roll gives you one of two benefits:

| Option                | Effect                                                                                         |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Automatic success** | Gain one automatic success on your roll (does not count as a die, cannot be cancelled by ones) |
| **Bonus dice**        | Add a number of dice to your pool equal to your **Passion** virtue                             |

<TWWrapper>
    <InlineRoll notation={'8d10\u003e=6f=1'} variant="details" preroll /> — a Dexterity 3 +
    Athletics 1 pool boosted by Passion 4 = 8d10
</TWWrapper>

Force Points are regained through roleplaying your character's Nature, exactly like Willpower in V:TM.

### Willpower

Unlike V:TM, **Willpower points cannot be spent** for automatic successes. Instead, Willpower is a **static defense** — it sets the target number for resisted actions against your character (e.g., someone using Intimidation, interrogation, or Force powers against you).

Your starting Willpower equals **Passion + Self-Control** (or 6 in games without Force users).

## 6. Modifying the Pool

Many factors adjust your effective dice pool before you roll.

### Wound Penalties

As damage accumulates, your performance degrades:

| Health Level  | Penalty   |
| ------------- | --------- |
| Bruised       | —         |
| Hurt          | **-1**    |
| Injured       | **-2**    |
| Wounded       | **-3**    |
| Mauled        | **-4**    |
| Crippled      | **-5**    |
| Incapacitated | Can't act |

These dice are subtracted directly from every action pool. A Wounded character (‑3) with Dexterity 5 + Blaster 3 = 8 dice rolls only **5 dice**. The pain is real.

### Splitting Pools for Multiple Actions

To do more than one thing in a turn (e.g., shoot and dodge, or shoot twice at different targets), you must **split your dice pool**:

1. Identify your **smallest** relevant pool among the actions (use the same Attribute/Ability combo for each).
2. Divide that pool **as evenly as possible** among the actions.
3. Each action uses a separate roll with its allocated dice.

Movement costs **1 die per 3 yards** from whichever pool you choose.

**Example:** _Jax_ wants to shoot one stormtrooper (Dex 5 + Blaster 3 = 8 dice) and shoot another (same 8 dice). His smallest pool is 8. Divided two ways: **4 dice for the first shot, 4 dice for the second**. He makes two separate rolls.

### Untrained Actions

If you lack the relevant Ability, you may roll just your **Attribute** — raw talent without technique.

**When it's appropriate:**

- **Talents** — anyone can try to be intimidating (Charisma alone) or notice something suspicious (Perception alone). Training helps, but instinct works.
- **Skills** — anyone can pick up a blaster and pull the trigger (Dexterity alone) or attempt to ride a tamed tauntaun (Dexterity alone). The results won't be pretty, but it's possible.

**When the GM should forbid it:**

- **Knowledges** — you cannot attempt Astrogation without training (you'd hyperspace into a star). You cannot perform surgery without Medicine. You cannot slice an Imperial mainframe without Interface. These fields require specialized knowledge, not just raw intelligence.

**Examples:**

| Task                                          | Attribute Only?           | Reason                                                                     |
| --------------------------------------------- | ------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Intimidate a captive into talking             | **Yes** (Charisma alone)  | You can shout and threaten; training just makes it more effective          |
| Fire a blaster rifle for the first time       | **Yes** (Dexterity alone) | Point and shoot — you might miss, but the trigger works                    |
| Pilot a starfighter through an asteroid field | **No**                    | Without Pilot, you don't know how the controls map to movement             |
| Translate an ancient Sith inscription         | **No**                    | Without Languages or Cultures, you can't even identify the script          |
| Climb a crumbling cliff face                  | **Yes** (Strength alone)  | You grab handholds and pull — Athletics training makes it safer and faster |
| Hack a sealed Imperial terminal               | **No**                    | Without Interface, you don't know where to start or what to touch          |

### Environmental & Situational Modifiers

The Storyteller can add or subtract dice (or shift difficulty) based on any narrative factor. These are guidelines, not rigid rules:

| Situation                                                    | Modifier                               | Why                                                 |
| ------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Shooting from a moving speeder at full throttle              | **-2 dice**                            | The bumps, wind, and vibration ruin your aim        |
| Firing from behind reinforced cover (only your head exposed) | **+1 difficulty** (for the attacker)   | Smaller target, harder to hit                       |
| Trying to negotiate in a deafening cantina                   | **+1 difficulty** on social rolls      | You can barely hear yourself think                  |
| Using a familiar tool in perfect conditions                  | **-1 difficulty**                      | Your own workshop, your own blaster, your own chair |
| Operating on a patient while the ship is taking fire         | **-3 dice** to Medicine                | Every tremor could cost a life                      |
| Searching a pitch-black room with only a tiny glow-rod       | **+2 difficulty**                      | You're working in near-darkness                     |
| Running on ice during a firefight                            | **-1 die** to all physical pools       | Your footing is unreliable                          |
| Studying a datapad in a quiet library with unlimited time    | **Automatic success** (if pool ≥ diff) | No pressure, perfect conditions                     |

### Specialization, Practiced & Experienced (SPE)

Each Ability can carry up to three **SPE flags** — toggles representing focused expertise, hard-won wisdom, or relentless practice. These appear as `S`, `P`, and `E` buttons above the dot rating on the character sheet.

#### Specialization (S) — Exploding 10s

Every Ability can have a **specialization** — a focused sub-category written into the field below the trait name (e.g., **Blaster: blaster pistols**, **Pilot: starfighters**, **Diplomacy: bribery**). When you roll within your declared specialization, your **10s explode** — each time you roll a 10, reroll that die and add the result. You keep the success the 10 originally indicated and may roll again for another success, continuing as long as you keep rolling 10s.

<TWWrapper>
    <InlineRoll notation={'5d10\u003e=6f=1!'} variant="details" preroll />
</TWWrapper>

A Dexterity 3 + Blaster 2 pool with Blaster (blaster pistols) specialization rolls `5d10>=6f=1!` — the `!` makes every natural 10 explode.

The GM decides if your specialization applies. A character with "Pilot: starfighters" does not get exploding dice when flying a bulk freighter.

#### Experienced (E) — Ignore One `1`

You have made enough mistakes with this Ability that you can recognize them in time. When you roll this Ability, you may **ignore a single `1` result** — one die showing `1` is set aside and does not trigger the Rule of One.

<TWWrapper>
    <InlineRoll notation={'5d10\u003e=6@2,7,4,1,9'} variant="details" preroll />
</TWWrapper>

This roll has 2 successes (`7`, `9`) and one `1`. Normally that `1` cancels one success, leaving 1. With **Experienced**, the `1` is ignored — the result stays at **2 successes**.

Experienced is a 1-point Merit and can be taken multiple times (each for a different Ability).

#### Practiced (P) — Round Up When Splitting

You have spent thousands of hours honing this Ability until it becomes second nature. Any time you **split your dice pool** and this Ability is involved, you **round up** your share instead of down when dividing dice.

**Example:** _Duhla_ has Dexterity 4 + Blaster 4 = 8 dice. She wants to shoot two stormtroopers and dive behind cover — three actions. 8 ÷ 3 = 2.66 dice per action, normally rounded down to **2 dice each**. With **Practiced: Blaster**, her Blaster share rounds up to **3 dice** — the extra efficiency comes from muscle memory honed through thousands of repetitions.

Practiced is a 1-point Merit and can be taken multiple times (each for a different Ability).

## 7. Soak Rolls & Damage Types

When an attack lands, roll **Stamina** at difficulty 6 to soak. Each success cancels one health level of damage.

| Damage Type                                    | Can Soak?                                                                           | Minimum                                                 | Recovery         |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------- |
| **Bashing** (fists, falls, blunt)              | Everyone                                                                            | None                                                    | 1 level/hour     |
| **Lethal** (blasters, lightsabers, explosives) | **Only PCs and named NPCs** — the **Heroic Rule Barrier**. Fodder NPCs cannot soak. | **Minimum 1** — even a perfect soak still takes 1 wound | 1 day–5 months\* |
| **Stun** (stun setting, ion vs. droids)        | Everyone (soaked like Bashing)                                                      | None                                                    | 1 level/turn     |

_\* Medical attention shortens recovery significantly._

**Stun** replaces V:TM's Aggravated damage. Marked as dots (`•`). If Stun fills the track to **Incapacitated**, the character falls unconscious for **1d10 minutes**. Bashing/Lethal taken while stunned halts quick recovery.

**Botching on Soak:** Overrides the WoD exception that soak rolls can't botch. If you botch any Soak roll, you fall unconscious (or reboot, if a Droid) for **1d10 minutes** from systemic shock, regardless of the damage value.

<TWWrapper>
    <InlineRoll notation={'3d10\u003e=6'} variant="details" preroll /> — Stamina 3 soak against
    Lethal. Each success cancels one level, but minimum 1 wound gets through.
</TWWrapper>

For full details on soak, damage, healing, and the Heroic Rule Barrier, see **[Health & Damage](/docs/star-wars-wod-2e/combat/health-damage-heal)**.
